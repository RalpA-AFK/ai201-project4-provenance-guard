# Provenance Guard

A service that assesses whether text-based content was written by a human or
generated by AI, and communicates that verdict with honest uncertainty. Built
for a writing platform, where wrongly labeling a human's work as AI is the most
costly error — so confidence scoring, label wording, and an appeals path are all
designed around protecting the creator.

## Architecture (submission → label)

Text is submitted to `POST /submit`. It runs through two independent detection
signals, whose outputs are combined into a single confidence score. That score
is mapped to a plain-language transparency label, and the whole decision is
written to an audit log before the response is returned. Creators who disagree
can contest the verdict via `POST /appeal`, which logs their reasoning alongside
the original decision and moves the content to "under review". See
[planning.md](planning.md) for the full architecture narrative and flow diagram.

## Web interface

Beyond the JSON API, the service serves a browser UI at the root (`GET /`,
[templates/index.html](templates/index.html)): paste text, click **Analyze**, and
see the verdict, a 0→1 AI-likelihood meter, and the full "why" — both signal
scores, the language model's written rationale, the stylometry metrics, and a
plain-English note on whether the signals agreed or disagreed. Each result also
has a **"Disagree with this result? Appeal it"** control that files an appeal
(moving the content to `under_review`) without leaving the page. Start the server
and open <http://localhost:5000/>.

## Detection signals

Two *distinct* signals — one semantic, one structural. They are independent
because one reads meaning and the other measures form, so together they are more
informative than either alone.

**Why this pairing.** The two signals fail in *different* directions, which is the
whole point. The LLM is strong on meaning but can be fooled by lightly edited AI
text and is non-deterministic; stylometry is deterministic and cheap but blind to
meaning and easily misled by formal human writing. Pairing them means a confident
verdict requires *both* a semantic and a structural reason to agree — and when
they don't, the system says "uncertain" instead of guessing. Two variants of the
same approach (e.g. two LLM prompts) would share the same blind spots and add no
real independence.

1. **LLM classification (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile)** — asks the model to
   judge holistically whether the text reads as human- or AI-written.
   - *Measures:* semantic and stylistic coherence.
   - *Why it differs:* AI prose is often fluent but generic and evenly weighted;
     human writing takes odder turns and uneven emphasis.
   - *Blind spot:* can be fooled by lightly edited AI text; non-deterministic, so
     the same text may score slightly differently across calls.

2. **Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)** — measurable statistical properties:
   sentence-length variation (coefficient of variation), average word length, and
   long-word fraction (share of words ≥ 7 characters).
   - *Measures:* structural uniformity and lexical sophistication of the writing.
   - *Why it differs:* AI/formal prose tends to be uniform and uses longer, more
     sophisticated words; casual human writing varies more and uses shorter words.
   - *Blind spot:* short texts produce unstable statistics; formal or polished
     human writing looks AI-like on lexical metrics (handled by letting signal
     disagreement pull the result into "uncertain").

## Confidence scoring

The two signals each emit `p_ai` (probability the text is AI-generated) in
`[0,1]`. They are combined in [scoring.py](scoring.py) `combine()`:

```
base     = 0.6 * llm_p + 0.4 * stylometry_p     # LLM weighted higher (more reliable)
d        = |llm_p - stylometry_p|               # how much the signals disagree
ai_score = base * (1 - d) + 0.5 * d             # disagreement pulls toward 0.5
```

**What the score means.** `ai_score` is the system's estimated probability the
text is AI-generated. We decided the *meaning of the middle first*: **`0.5` means
"we are refusing to guess,"** not "50% AI." A `0.6` means "leaning AI but not
confident — do not accuse." A `0.95` means "strong, agreeing evidence of AI." The
disagreement term is the key idea: when the semantic and structural signals
conflict, the score is dragged toward the uncertain middle, so a single noisy
signal can't produce a confident (possibly false-positive) verdict.

**Thresholds are asymmetric on purpose.** Because labeling a human's work as AI
is the worst error, the AI band demands more evidence than the human band:

| Band | Condition | Attribution |
|---|---|---|
| High-confidence AI | `ai_score ≥ 0.80` | `likely_ai` |
| Uncertain | `0.30 < ai_score < 0.80` | `uncertain` |
| High-confidence human | `ai_score ≤ 0.30` | `likely_human` |

A reader-facing `confidence` = `2 × |ai_score − 0.5|` is also returned (0 at the
uncertain middle, 1 at either extreme).

**How I tested the scores are meaningful.** I ran four deliberately chosen inputs
(clearly AI, clearly human, formal human, lightly-edited AI) and printed each
signal separately. This caught a real bug: my original stylometry metrics
(type-token ratio, punctuation density) didn't discriminate at typical
submission length (~40–60 words) — every sample scored 0.87–0.98 on TTR — which
pushed a clearly-AI sample into "uncertain." I replaced them with average word
length and long-word fraction and recalibrated. Final results:

| Input | LLM | Stylometry | Combined | Band |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clearly AI | 0.80 | 1.00 | 0.80 | `likely_ai` |
| Clearly human | 0.20 | 0.04 | 0.19 | `likely_human` |
| Formal human | 0.70 | 0.88 | 0.72 | `uncertain` (protects the human) |
| Lightly-edited AI | 0.20 | 0.51 | 0.38 | `uncertain` (mid-range) |

### Two example submissions (meaningful variation)

The reader-facing `confidence` is `2 × |ai_score − 0.5|`. These two real
submissions show the score is not a constant — it moves with the evidence:

**Higher-confidence case** — casual first-person text:
> *"ok so i finally tried that new ramen place downtown and honestly? underwhelming.
> the broth was fine but WAY too much sodium and i was thirsty for like three hours
> after. probably wont go back unless someone drags me"*

`llm_score = 0.20`, `stylometry_score = 0.04` → `ai_score = 0.19`,
**`confidence = 0.61`** → **Likely human-written**. Both signals agree it reads
human, so the score sits confidently in the human band.

**Lower-confidence case** — formal human writing on economics:
> *"The relationship between monetary policy and asset price inflation has been
> extensively studied in the literature. Central banks face a fundamental tension
> between price stability and prolonged low interest rates."*

`llm_score = 0.70`, `stylometry_score = 0.88` → `ai_score = 0.72`,
**`confidence = 0.45`** → **Uncertain**. Both signals lean AI, but the writing is
genuinely human — formal prose that *looks* machine-made. The system correctly
declines to call it AI and lands in the uncertain band, exactly the false-positive
protection the design is built around.

## Transparency labels

The label returned by `/submit` changes with the `ai_score` band. Exact text:

**High-confidence AI** (`ai_score ≥ 0.80`):
> ⚠️ Likely AI-generated. Our automated analysis found strong signs that this
> text was created with significant AI assistance. Automated detection is
> imperfect and can be wrong — if you're the author and disagree, you can appeal
> this label.

**High-confidence human** (`ai_score ≤ 0.30`):
> ✓ Likely human-written. Our automated analysis found no strong signs of AI
> generation in this text. Automated checks aren't perfect, but this content
> reads as human-written.

**Uncertain** (`0.30 < ai_score < 0.80`):
> ❓ Not enough evidence to say. Our automated checks were inconclusive or
> disagreed for this text, so we won't guess whether it was written by a human or
> AI. If you're the author, you can add context through an appeal.

The AI label is deliberately non-accusatory ("found signs," "can be wrong") and
always names the appeal path; the uncertain label frames refusing to guess as an
intentional choice, not a failure.

## Appeals workflow

A creator contests a verdict with `POST /appeal`, providing the `content_id` from
their `/submit` response and free-text `creator_reasoning`. The endpoint:

1. Looks up the original decision (404 if the `content_id` is unknown).
2. Updates the content's status from `classified` → `under_review`.
3. Logs the appeal (reasoning + timestamp) alongside the original decision.
4. Returns a confirmation. **No automated re-classification** — a human moderator
   reviews the queue, where they see the original text, both signal scores, the
   LLM rationale, and the creator's reasoning.

```bash
curl -s -X POST http://localhost:5000/appeal \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"content_id": "PASTE-ID", "creator_reasoning": "I wrote this myself..."}'
# -> { "content_id": "...", "status": "under_review", "message": "Appeal received..." }
```

## Rate limiting

Applied to `POST /submit` via Flask-Limiter (in-memory storage): **`10 per
minute; 100 per day`** per client IP.

- **10/minute:** no genuine writer submits more than ~10 distinct pieces in a
  minute — even rapid re-submissions after edits stay well under this — but a
  flood script trips it immediately. It also protects the Groq quota, since every
  submission is one LLM call.
- **100/day:** gives a prolific writer or a small editorial team comfortable
  headroom while capping a slow-drip abuse pattern that stays under the
  per-minute limit, and bounds daily API cost.

Every submission and appeal is recorded. Each entry captures: `content_id`,
`creator_id`, `timestamp`, `attribution`, `confidence`, combined `ai_score`, both
individual signal scores (`llm_score`, `stylometry_score`), full per-signal
detail, `status`, and appeal fields (`appeal_filed`, `appeal_reasoning`).

## API

| Method | Path      | Accepts                                              | Returns                                                    |
|--------|-----------|-----------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------|
| POST   | `/submit` | `{ "text": "...", "creator_id": "..." }`            | `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, `ai_score`, `label`, `signals` |
| POST   | `/appeal` | `{ "content_id": "...", "creator_reasoning": "..." }`| `content_id`, `status` (`under_review`), `message`         |
| GET    | `/log`    | —                                                   | `{ "entries": [ ... ] }` structured audit log             |

Errors: `400` (missing/empty fields), `404` (unknown appeal `content_id`),
`429` (rate limit exceeded).

## Known limitations

Perfect AI detection is unsolved; this system is built to fail *honestly* rather
than confidently. Specific cases it will get wrong, tied to signal properties:

- **Formal or academic human writing** is the clearest failure mode. Every one of
  my stylometric metrics (long, sophisticated words; low sentence-length
  variation) points the same way for a careful human essay as it does for AI —
  they measure *form*, and formal human form genuinely resembles AI form. The LLM
  usually pushes back, so these land in "uncertain" rather than a false "AI," but
  that means a legitimate formal writer often can't earn a confident
  "human-written" label. That's a deliberate trade (uncertain beats false
  accusation), but it *is* a limitation.
- **Non-native English writers** hit the same edge: simpler or more uniform
  sentence structure can read as AI-like on the stylometric signal. This is
  exactly the scenario the appeals workflow exists for.
- **Short submissions (< ~40 words)** — a haiku or a two-line excerpt gives
  stylometry too little data for stable statistics, so its score is pulled toward
  0.5 and the LLM effectively decides alone, collapsing the system back to a
  single signal.
- **Lightly human-edited AI text** reads human to both signals and will often be
  called human. On a writing platform this is the *acceptable* error direction (a
  false negative, not a false positive).

## What I'd change for a real deployment

- **Calibrate on real data.** My stylometric thresholds were tuned against a
  handful of sample texts — legitimate for a prototype, but a real system needs
  thresholds fit against a labeled corpus, ideally per-genre (poetry vs. essays
  vs. casual posts have different baselines).
- **Persist rate-limit state.** In-memory storage resets on restart and doesn't
  work across multiple processes; production would use Redis.
- **Authenticate appeals and `/log`.** Right now anyone with a `content_id` can
  appeal and `/log` is open — fine for grading, unacceptable in production.
- **Make the LLM signal reproducible.** Cache verdicts by text hash so the same
  submission can't get different scores on re-run, and log the model version.

## Spec reflection

- **How the spec helped:** writing the confidence-scoring section in
  [planning.md](planning.md) *before* coding forced me to decide what `0.5` means
  to a user (refusing to guess) and to invent the disagreement term up front.
  Because that was settled in prose, the implementation was a direct translation —
  I never had to retrofit uncertainty handling after the fact, and the asymmetric
  thresholds fell out naturally from the "false positives are worst" principle.
- **How the implementation diverged:** the spec named type-token ratio and
  punctuation density as two of the stylometric metrics. During Milestone 4
  testing they turned out not to discriminate at typical submission length (every
  sample scored 0.87–0.98 on TTR), so I replaced them with average word length and
  long-word fraction and recalibrated. The divergence was driven by data, not
  convenience, and I updated planning.md to record why.

## AI usage

- **Stylometry calibration debugging.** I directed an AI tool to help diagnose why
  a clearly-AI sample was scoring "uncertain." It correctly identified that TTR is
  length-dependent and clusters high on short texts. I took the diagnosis but
  *overrode* its first fix (a fixed offset on the TTR score, which would have been
  a hack) in favor of replacing the metric entirely with length-robust ones I
  verified against the sample data myself.
- **Flask/`/appeal` scaffolding.** I had an AI tool draft the `/appeal` route and
  the SQLite appeal-logging function from my planning.md appeals section. My spec
  used the field names `{id, reason}` and status `"under review"`; I revised the
  implementation to the exact contract the graders test against
  (`content_id`, `creator_reasoning`, `under_review`), added a 404 path for an
  unknown `content_id`, and updated planning.md to match so spec and code stay in
  sync.
